Effective Communication
What is the specified purpose of the site?
    The site is intended as a useful resource, social area and communication avenue for parents of all types. Created by parents for parents.
What information is the site attempting to communicate?
    Information that is useful in many aspects of raising and dealing with children, as well as promoting open communication with other parents and specialists. The content is a resource for parents to turn to when they need help, advice or direction, and overall is intended to communicate that help and advice is just a click away.
What types of users did you identify for the site?

Using the method of User Profiling, (introducted in Unit 8), I constructed a number of user profiles which encouraged consideration of the extremes of parenting. Combining this with discussions with other parents and my wife, a professional childminder, I  was able to identify a wide range of users.  Parents of all types and from all classes; young or old, working or unemployed, stressed or care-free,  busy or under-utilized, able-bodied or disabled in some form, foreign nationalities, single, married or divorcees, step or foster parents or even carers. Also parents who deal with looking after different types of children; one child or many children, disabled children, shy or hyperactive children.

How did you ensure that your site matched user characteristics?

Again taking the lead from Unit 8, I took 2 user-trips through the site, recording and correcting obvious deficiencies. I also asked my wife and a friend to take a trip through it and made minor adjustments based on their experience. Overall I attemped to follow the guidlines on usability and accessability, while also taking care with colours, font, images and clear simple text.

What interactive features could usefully be added to your site?
Unit 9 gave a good introduction to this, and the ideas of direct manipulation (for user choice) and form filling would prove a useful starting point.  However, further research and learning would be needed before I could undertake this task.
    A feedback sheet would help with continual improvement of the site inline with user experience. However, more importantly, would be the inclusion of the facility for direct interaction with other parents, and possibly with professionals via online appointment booking method or timed availability for question and answer sessions. This interactivity of an open chat room for parents, with the availability of confidential sesssions with professionals, would likely be the site's main attraction. Although search facilities can prove useful I would not include at this stage, as the site is small and simple.

Site design, ease of navigation and accessibility

How did you decide the structure of your site?

Following the guidelines in Unit 7 I first drew up a written "requirements specification" for the site. From this I then summarised the main information areas, looking at other sites for inspiration, and worked on organising this information into a hierarachal tree structure, limiting the depth to 4 levels. On review of Unit 2 I concluded the tree structure was the best option for this initial outlay.  

How did you organise or 'chunk' the information to be put on individual pages?

Again Unit 7 proved helpful, in using my initial tree structure to develop a site diagram on paper, showing the initial ideas of pages and content, working down from the home page.  Overall it was difficult to seperate the information, but I narrowed it down to 5 main categories, in order to meet the limitation of only 5 additional pages being allowed for the ECA.

What consideration did you give to navigation and usability?

Unit 7 taught that a consistent visual appearance for each page would be essential for usability and navigation. From this I decided that a logo and a page title on each page would be best, in addition to the consistent format of each page (layout, colour, images etc) across the site that I had also picked up from the course material and by looking at other web sites. 

In Unit 8, Ex3.3, the materials author created a site, from which I liked the idea of the links being on top of the page, as opposed to the side of the page.  With ony 5 linked pages for the site I found this would work well for my site and kept with this, while keeping other useful links to the left panel of each page.

I also learnt from Unit 7 that location information was important in helping the user to identify where they are.  With this in mind I created seperate location links at the top left of each page, which also accommodated return to the home page from any other page.

I considered a site map but decided against it, concluding that the site was too small and simple to need this.

In designing the site, what considerations did you give to accessibility?

Using Unit 8 as a guide I have kept the design simple and clear, attempting to maintain good contrast between background colour and text throughout, while also keeping images simple and ensuring that they have associated altenative text to aid screen readers. I have also kept away from animations and limited scrolling on all sites, using links and anchors where needed.

I attempted to use some of the accessibility checking methods showing in Unit 8, but found installing the text browser to complicated and the html references in the validation sites too much for my limited knowledge. I put the latter part partly down to the underlying HTML code generated by NVU.
How did you develop and test your site?

I developed my site using NVU as recommended by the course and tested through my firefox browser and Internet Explorer. Although there were some differences with the display in IE I decided not to work on fixing these as the ECA did not require this.

Page design, layout, use of fonts, use of images, use of colour

How did you decide the page layout for your site?
    Looking at other parental sites the commonality seemed to be a table layout of 2 or 3 columns, a guideline which I followed.
How did you position the elements of your web pages?

I positioned the logo top left of each page and the title top center, in line with the most common layout I perceived on other sites. As there was only 5 main page links I decided these would look better, and would provide the best usability, at the top of the page, as opposed to the side of the page.

I then toyed with different uses of the side columns, concluding that the left bar would be most useful for other user-centric information, such as exterior links and contacts, and the right would be best used for the image display.

Overall I concluded that the same layout throughout would keep the simplicity I aimed for, while improving the navigation and usability of the site.

Which fonts and sizes did you use and why?

Units 1-3 where useful here, particularly the condensed guidelines and the information on combining text with images in Unit 1. I didnt want to stray too far from the recommended font of Verdana, (one of the 10 golden rules of Unit 3), as I found it to be very readable.  However, using it throughout gave a bland look to the overall site, so I decided to introduce a 'fun' font for the Site Name and the main links at the top of the page.  The font I found worked best was "Kristen ITC", which I have used, but for the Site's name I opted to create a graphic version (via screen dump to paint.exe), for the added reassurance that its appearance would not change when viewed in other browsers.  I also worked with the idea of changing the links at the top of the page to graphical alternatives, but this didnt work as well, as this took away from their hyperlink appearance, detracting from the sites usability.

Explain your choice of graphic file types (JPEG, GIF, etc.) to suit your images?

The intention was to create a relaxed welcoming look, nothing too serious or overly professional, while also wanting to keep a simplistic and happy look and feel about it. To this end I choose to keep images simple, staying away from photos or complex images and keeping with simple, (humorously) light images.  I also wanted to minimise colourful images to avoid conflict with the overall colourscheme and warmth of feeling.

Unit 5 of the course material provided some excellent advice on the use of images for web pages, and from this I learnt that the most suitable format for my simple images, was either GIFF or PNG format.  I eventually opted to save all my images in PNG format, as it resulted in better quality and smaller file sizes than GIFF.

Explain the reasoning behind your choice of colour scheme in the web pages.
On investigating other Parental sites I found simple 2-colour palettes to be the norm, with orange and green colours appearing common. (provide 2 example sites).  I wanted to keep a warm welcoming feel to the site and with this in mind kept to a simple , 2-colour, warm palette of orange and yellow mainly throughout.

Web editing tools

Critique of Nvu;
The editor should enable a complete beginner to make a web page in an hour or two.

Good features
  • Simple and easy use
  • Preview facility
  • Good useful shortcuts
  • GUI interface with familiar menu structure and usability
Bad Features
  • Cells confusing to format as limited to overall table format
  • No messages pop up to report of errors, it either ignores request or crashes.
  • Menu items can take a bit of getting used to
  • What you see is not always what you get
It should not require any technical knowledge about coding languages.
Good Features
  • GUI interface
  • Menu options and shortcuts accommodate full page creation
  • Generates HTML code in background
Bad Features
  • HTML code generated does not display the same in all browsers.
  • Results of validating may require manual code adjustment
It should be available free and should enable students to complete the T183 ECA.
Good features
  • It’s free
  • Enabled completion of a basic site for ECA
Bad features
  • You get what you pay for, basic features


Evaluation comments
I found NVU to be easy to use and as a beginner I was able to build a website fairly quickly using its user friendly interface and relatively familiar menu structure. Working with images was easy but working with text was more restricted, with limited range of type size allowed. Using tables proved frustrating, due to it continually crashing and not informing of inaction.

I may continue using to build up experience but would definitely look for alternative as would like to gain more familiarity with the HTML.  How NVU incorporates or accommodates CSS style sheets, xml, xhtml or code validation is unclear and I would be interested in exploring this more.
